Research

Five interconnected areas. One coherent question.

I

Public Perception & Awareness of AI Across Africa

Using Google Trends data spanning 2019–2023 across 50 African countries to understand structural engagement patterns. What I found was not simply a digital divide but a structural problem: AI interest spiked around global events like ChatGPT's launch, but the baseline engagement across most African countries remained low and uneven — mapped directly onto infrastructure gaps, policy environments, and colonial legacies.

Built in collaboration with Dr. Chinasa T. Okolo. The resulting dataset, AfriAI-Trends, was accepted at EAAMO 2024.

II

AI's Role in Disinformation & Democratic Integrity

Examining how AI can simultaneously detect and amplify disinformation. I led work on audio-visual inconsistency detection, source credibility analysis, and the specific vulnerabilities of African democracies: linguistic diversity, weak digital infrastructure, and low institutional capacity to respond.

Deploying AI in fragile political contexts without understanding those contexts is not neutral. It is a choice, and it has consequences for people.

III

AI Adoption, Regulation & Ethics Dumping

As lead author at the Equiano Institute, I examined policy, educational, and infrastructural barriers to AI adoption while addressing risks like data bias and what we called ethics dumping — the practice of exporting AI systems built under one set of ethical assumptions into contexts where those assumptions don't hold.

Presented at the Data Community Africa Conference, Lagos, 2024.

IV

Language Inclusivity & Fairness in NLP

In collaboration with Professor Adegboyega Ojo, I'm testing how large language models handle Igbo — spoken by ~30 million people, classified in the left-behind resource tier. Even when these models sound fluent, they consistently miss semantic and cultural context.

The Igbo words Lolo and Nwoye Igwe carry distinct cultural weight — one about status, one about lineage — but GPT-4 translates them as equivalent, stripping away emotional and cultural layers. Fairness in NLP is not about error rates. It is about whether a system recognises that a speaker's meaning exists at all.

V

Invisible Failure Modes in AI Systems

My working manuscript, submitted to AIES 2026, makes a core distinction: AI governance has a sophisticated vocabulary for detecting bias — we know how to find it when a system treats one recognised group worse than another. But there is a more fundamental failure that comes before bias can even be measured.

Exclusion is being treated unequally inside the room. Omission is never being allowed into the building. Our entire governance architecture is designed to audit what happens inside the room.

→

The ethical and the technical collapse into one another.

Translating a harmful prompt into a low-resource language — Zulu, Scottish Gaelic, Hmong — raises GPT-4's safety filter bypass rate from below 1% to approximately 79%.

The communities most harmed by language omission as a matter of justice are simultaneously the communities whose linguistic context is most easily weaponised against everyone. Omission is not only an injustice. It is a security vulnerability.
